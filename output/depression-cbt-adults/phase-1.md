## Abstract

### Item 1: Title Identification

This is a systematic review and meta-analysis of randomized controlled trials evaluating the efficacy of cognitive behavioral therapy (CBT) compared to standard care, placebo, attention control, other psychotherapies, or waitlist control for treating major depressive disorder in adults aged 18 years or older. The review examines depression symptom severity, remission rates, and response rates as primary outcomes.

### Item 2: Background/Rationale

Major depressive disorder (MDD) is a leading cause of disability worldwide, and cognitive behavioral therapy (CBT) has emerged as a first-line psychological intervention; however, the relative efficacy of CBT across diverse delivery formats and comparative contexts remains uncertain, necessitating an updated synthesis of evidence from randomized controlled trials.

### Item 3: Objectives

This systematic review aims to evaluate the efficacy of cognitive behavioral therapy compared to standard care, placebo, attention control, other psychotherapies, and waitlist control in reducing depression symptom severity and achieving remission in adults (aged ≥18 years) with major depressive disorder. The research question is: What is the effect size of CBT relative to comparator interventions on depression symptom reduction, remission rates, and response rates in this population?

### Item 4: Eligibility Criteria

**Study design**: Randomized controlled trials (RCTs) published in English from 2010 onwards with a minimum sample size of 20 participants.

**Population**: Adults aged 18 years or older with a diagnosis of major depressive disorder according to DSM-5 or ICD-11 criteria, of any severity level, in any treatment setting (primary care, outpatient mental health, inpatient).

**Intervention**: Cognitive behavioral therapy delivered individually or in group format, of any duration and frequency, via any delivery method (face-to-face, teletherapy, digital).

**Comparators**: Standard/usual care, placebo, attention control, other structured psychotherapies (psychodynamic therapy, interpersonal therapy, supportive therapy), or waitlist control.

**Outcomes**: Primary outcomes include depression symptom severity (validated scales: PHQ-9, BDI-II, HDRS, or equivalent), remission rates (≥50% reduction or full remission from baseline), and response rates. Secondary outcomes include quality of life, functional impairment, relapse/recurrence rates, treatment dropout rates, and adverse events.

**Exclusion criteria**: Studies exclusively in populations with bipolar disorder, psychotic disorders, or active substance use disorders; studies without a comparison group; case reports, editorials, and commentaries.

### Item 6: Risk of Bias Methods

**Bias assessment tool**: Risk of bias will be assessed at the study level using the Cochrane Risk of Bias tool (RoB 2), evaluating five domains: bias arising from the randomization process, bias due to deviations from the intended intervention, bias due to missing outcome data, bias in outcome measurement, and bias in the selection of reported results.

**Quality assurance**: Two independent reviewers will assess each study; disagreements will be resolved by consensus or referral to a third reviewer.

### Item 7: Synthesis Methods

**Synthesis approach**: Meta-analysis of randomized controlled trials using random-effects models to estimate pooled effect sizes (Hedges' g standardized mean differences) due to anticipated heterogeneity in intervention delivery, comparator type, outcome measurement, and study population.

**Sensitivity and subgroup analyses**: Planned subgroup analyses include delivery format (face-to-face vs. digital), comparator type (standard care vs. active psychological comparators), depression severity at baseline (mild/moderate vs. severe), and publication year (2010–2015 vs. 2016 onwards).

### Item 10: Limitations

**Evidence base limitations**: Limitations may include potential publication bias favoring positive findings, heterogeneity in outcome measurement and depression severity definitions across trials, and limited representation of certain populations (e.g., severe depression, older adults, specific ethnic/racial groups).

**Review process limitations**: The review is restricted to English-language publications from 2010 onwards, which may exclude relevant international evidence; additionally, the quality and completeness of outcome reporting in included studies and the statistical heterogeneity of treatment effects may limit the precision of pooled estimates.
