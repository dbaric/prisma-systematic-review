# Phase 3: Methods — PRISMA Items 3–15

Cognitive Behavioral Therapy for Major Depressive Disorder in Adults: A Systematic Review and Meta-Analysis of Randomized Controlled Trials

---

## Eligibility Criteria

### Study Design
This systematic review will include randomized controlled trials (RCTs) that evaluate the efficacy of cognitive behavioral therapy (CBT) for major depressive disorder in adults. Only RCTs with a comparison group will be included. Eligible RCTs will have:
- A primary focus on CBT for depression (not as an adjunct to other primary interventions)
- A clearly randomized allocation process
- A defined comparator (standard care, placebo, active psychological comparator, waitlist, or treatment as usual)

**Publication status**: Peer-reviewed journal articles published in English from 2010 onwards. Secondary sources (including conference abstracts, theses/dissertations, and preprints meeting quality standards) will be considered if no published peer-reviewed version exists; inclusion criteria for non-peer-reviewed sources will be [SPECIFY: criteria for non-peer-reviewed inclusion].

### Population
- **Age**: Adults aged 18 years or older at study enrollment
- **Diagnosis**: Major depressive disorder (MDD) diagnosed per DSM-5 criteria or ICD-11 criteria, confirmed by validated diagnostic interview or clinical assessment
- **Severity**: Any severity level of depression at baseline (mild, moderate, or severe) as defined by study enrollment criteria or baseline score on validated depression scales
- **Setting**: Any treatment setting including primary care, outpatient mental health clinics, private practice, teletherapy platforms, community mental health centers, or inpatient psychiatric units
- **Exclusion**: Studies exclusively enrolling participants with bipolar disorder, psychotic disorders, or active substance use disorders as primary diagnoses will be excluded. Studies with mixed populations (e.g., depression + anxiety) will be included only if depression is the primary focus and data can be extracted separately or analyzed for the depression subgroup

### Intervention
**Cognitive behavioral therapy** defined as a structured, time-limited psychological intervention based on the theory that depressive symptoms result from maladaptive cognitions and behaviors, with the goal of identifying and modifying these patterns. Eligible CBT interventions will include:
- Individual or group delivery formats
- Any duration and frequency (e.g., weekly sessions, intensive formats)
- Any delivery method: face-to-face, video-based teletherapy, telephone-based, or fully digital/web-based platforms
- CBT delivered by licensed mental health professionals (psychiatrists, psychologists, counselors, clinical social workers) or trained therapists
- Manualised CBT or CBT adapted for digital delivery (e.g., guided internet-based CBT, unguided digital CBT with supportive contact)

**Exclusion**: Studies of CBT as an adjunct to pharmacological therapy will be excluded unless CBT versus control (without medication) can be extracted separately. Interventions labeled "CBT" but not meeting core cognitive-behavioral principles will be excluded.

### Comparators
Studies will be included if they compare CBT to one or more of the following:
1. **Standard/usual care**: Treatment as normally provided in a clinical setting (may include routine psychiatric care, primary care, or medication management without psychotherapy)
2. **Placebo or attention control**: Pill placebo, sham therapy, or matched attention without active therapeutic components
3. **Other active psychotherapies**: Psychodynamic psychotherapy, interpersonal therapy (IPT), supportive therapy, counseling, or behavioral therapy (not CBT)
4. **Waitlist control**: No treatment or delayed treatment
5. **Combination**: Studies with multiple comparators are eligible and will be analyzed in separate meta-analyses by comparator type

### Outcome Measures

#### Primary Outcomes
1. **Depression symptom severity** at end of treatment, measured by validated instruments:
   - Patient Health Questionnaire–9 (PHQ-9)
   - Beck Depression Inventory–II (BDI-II)
   - Hamilton Depression Rating Scale (HDRS/HAM-D)
   - Montgomery-Åsberg Depression Rating Scale (MADRS)
   - Other validated instruments (e.g., Zung Self-Rating Depression Scale)
   - Outcome definition: Continuous score or mean change from baseline

2. **Remission rate**: Proportion of participants achieving ≥50% reduction in depression symptom score from baseline to end of treatment, OR achievement of full remission (clinical definition to be applied consistently across studies)

3. **Response rate**: Proportion of participants achieving clinically meaningful response to treatment (definition standardized across studies, typically ≥50% reduction in symptom score or lower than threshold for clinical caseness)

#### Secondary Outcomes
1. **Quality of life**: Measured by validated instruments (e.g., Quality of Life Enjoyment and Satisfaction Questionnaire, WHO Quality of Life-BREF)
2. **Functional impairment**: Measured by validated scales (e.g., Sheehan Disability Scale, Work and Social Adjustment Scale)
3. **Relapse/recurrence rates**: Proportion of participants experiencing symptom return or disorder recurrence during follow-up period
4. **Treatment dropout rates**: Proportion of participants withdrawing from treatment for any reason
5. **Adverse events**: Types and frequencies of adverse effects reported

#### Time Points for Extraction
- **Primary time point**: End of active treatment (defined as last week of treatment intervention; [SPECIFY: how end of treatment will be operationalized if variable across studies])
- **Secondary time points**: 
  - Acute phase end (weeks [SPECIFY: 8-12] post-randomization if different from treatment endpoint)
  - Short-term follow-up ([SPECIFY: 3-6 months] post-treatment endpoint)
  - Intermediate follow-up ([SPECIFY: 6-12 months])
  - Long-term follow-up (12+ months)
- **Rule for multiple time points**: If a study reports multiple time points, the end-of-treatment time point will be the primary extraction. For subgroup analyses, the longest available follow-up within each time-point category will be extracted.

### Study Characteristics
- **Language**: Published in English (non-English publications will be translated if identified; [SPECIFY: translation methods and resources])
- **Publication year**: Published 2010 or later to capture contemporary CBT delivery methods and outcome measurement practices
- **Minimum sample size**: ≥20 participants per study arm at baseline (very small studies, defined as <20 per arm, will be excluded to reduce bias from extreme effects)
- **Complete outcome reporting**: Studies must report sufficient data (means, SDs, or frequencies) for at least one primary outcome to allow effect size calculation or must provide data upon author request

### Grouping for Synthesis
Studies will be organized for analysis according to:
1. **By comparator type** (separate meta-analyses):
   - CBT vs. standard care
   - CBT vs. placebo/attention control
   - CBT vs. other active psychotherapy
   - CBT vs. waitlist control

2. **By outcome domain** (separate meta-analyses):
   - Depression symptom severity (continuous outcome)
   - Remission rate (binary outcome)
   - Response rate (binary outcome)
   - Secondary outcomes (quality of life, functional impairment, dropout, relapse) if sufficient studies available

3. **By potential effect modifiers** (planned subgroup analyses):
   - CBT delivery format: face-to-face vs. digital/teletherapy
   - Depression severity at baseline: mild/moderate vs. severe
   - Treatment setting: primary care vs. specialist mental health
   - Study quality: low risk of bias vs. high/some risk of bias
   - Publication era: 2010–2015 vs. 2016–[current year]

## Risk of Bias Assessment

### Tool Selection
**Risk of Bias 2 (RoB 2)** for RCTs, developed by the Cochrane Collaboration, will be the primary tool for assessing study quality and risk of bias. This tool evaluates bias across five domains considered essential to trial validity.

**Justification**: RoB 2 is the current standard in Cochrane reviews, aligns with PRISMA 2020 requirements, and has been validated for comprehensive bias assessment in psychotherapy trials.

### Assessment Domains

1. **Bias arising from the randomization process** (Signaling questions):
   - Was the allocation sequence random?
   - Was the allocation sequence concealed until participants were enrolled and assigned to interventions?
   - Did baseline differences between groups suggest a problem with the randomization process?

2. **Bias due to deviations from the intended intervention** (Signaling questions):
   - Were participants aware of their assigned intervention?
   - Were intervention deliverers aware of participants' assigned interventions?
   - If deviations from intended intervention occurred, were they balanced between groups?
   - If there were deviations, were they likely to have affected the outcome?

3. **Bias due to missing outcome data** (Signaling questions):
   - Were outcome data available for all or nearly all participants randomized?
   - If data were missing, were they missing at random, and were appropriate methods used to account for missing data?
   - Is the proportion and nature of missing data consistent between intervention and comparator groups?

4. **Bias in the measurement of the outcome** (Signaling questions):
   - Was the outcome assessment blinded to intervention assignment?
   - If outcome assessment was not blinded, was there risk that knowledge of intervention assignment could have affected outcome measurement?
   - Were outcome assessment methods comparable between groups?

5. **Bias in the selection of the reported result** (Signaling questions):
   - Was the reported outcome a pre-specified primary outcome?
   - Was the numerical result reported exactly as analyzed?
   - Is the selection of reported results likely to have been driven by the magnitude or direction of effect?

### Judgment Framework
For each domain, one of the following judgments will be assigned:
- **Low risk of bias**: Signaling questions answered clearly with "Yes," indicating the domain has been well-managed
- **Some concerns**: One or more signaling questions answered with "Probably yes" or uncertainty exists, or there is ambiguity in domain-specific factors
- **High risk of bias**: One or more signaling questions answered "No," or evidence of bias is clear in that domain

**Overall risk of bias for each study** will be assigned as:
- **Low risk**: Low risk across all five domains
- **Some concerns**: Some concerns in ≥1 domain with low risk in others, or high risk in only one domain assessed as lower priority
- **High risk**: High risk in ≥1 domain assessed as critical (typically randomization, allocation concealment, or attrition) OR high risk in ≥2 domains

### Assessment Methods

**Structured approach**: Each domain will be assessed using the published RoB 2 algorithm, which provides a systematic method for moving from signaling question responses to domain-level risk judgments.

**Contextualization for psychotherapy trials**: Domain-specific considerations for psychotherapy trials will include:
- **Blinding**: Recognition that participant and therapist blinding to psychological interventions is not possible, but assessor blinding for outcomes is critical
- **Fidelity**: Consideration of whether intervention was delivered as intended and whether deviations from the protocol were documented and addressed
- **Comparator specification**: Clarity about what comparator arms received (e.g., "standard care" must be sufficiently described)

### Number of Reviewers and Disagreement Resolution
- **Independent assessment**: All included studies will be independently assessed for risk of bias by two reviewers with training in systematic review methodology and familiarity with RoB 2 tool
- **Reviewer qualifications**: [SPECIFY: degree level and experience of reviewers, e.g., "doctoral-level psychologists with prior RCT methodology training"]
- **Disagreement resolution**: Initial discrepancies between reviewers will be resolved by [SPECIFY: consensus discussion / third-party arbitration / consultation with senior methodologist]
- **Documentation**: The number and nature of disagreements and method of resolution will be reported in the final review

## Outcome Data Items

### Primary Outcomes

**Outcome 1: Depression Symptom Severity (Continuous)**
- **Definition**: Change from baseline or endpoint depression symptom score on validated rating scales
- **Instruments**: Patient Health Questionnaire–9 (PHQ-9), Beck Depression Inventory–II (BDI-II), Hamilton Depression Rating Scale (HDRS), Montgomery-Åsberg Depression Rating Scale (MADRS), or other validated instruments meeting psychometric standards (Cronbach's α ≥ 0.80)
- **Measurement**: Means and standard deviations (or medians/IQRs if means unavailable) at baseline and endpoint
- **Time points for extraction**:
  - Primary: End of treatment (defined as last session or final assessment during active treatment phase)
  - Secondary: Acute phase endpoint (weeks [SPECIFY: 8–12] post-randomization)
  - Short-term follow-up ([SPECIFY: 3–6 months]) post-treatment
  - Intermediate follow-up ([SPECIFY: 6–12 months])
  - Long-term follow-up (≥12 months)

**Outcome 2: Remission Rate (Binary)**
- **Definition**: Proportion of participants achieving ≥50% reduction in depression symptom score from baseline to endpoint, OR meeting predefined remission threshold on depression scale (e.g., BDI < 10, PHQ-9 < 5)
- **Study-specific criteria**: If studies define remission differently, the study's definition will be extracted and recorded; where heterogeneity allows, sensitivity analysis will explore impact of different remission definitions
- **Time points for extraction**: End of treatment (primary); other time points as above if reported

**Outcome 3: Response Rate (Binary)**
- **Definition**: Proportion of participants achieving clinically meaningful response to treatment, typically ≥50% symptom reduction or other study-defined response criterion
- **Standardization**: If studies use different response thresholds, the study definition will be extracted and noted; meta-analysis will use the outcome as reported by each study
- **Time points for extraction**: End of treatment (primary); other time points as above if reported

### Secondary Outcomes

**Outcome 4: Quality of Life**
- **Definition**: Participant-reported quality of life or life satisfaction
- **Instruments**: Quality of Life Enjoyment and Satisfaction Questionnaire (Q-LES-Q), WHO Quality of Life–BREF (WHOQOL-BREF), SF-36/SF-12 mental health component, or equivalent
- **Time points**: End of treatment and [SPECIFY: follow-up time points if available]

**Outcome 5: Functional Impairment**
- **Definition**: Degree of impairment in work, social, or interpersonal functioning
- **Instruments**: Sheehan Disability Scale, Work and Social Adjustment Scale (WSAS), Global Assessment of Functioning (GAF), or equivalent
- **Time points**: End of treatment and [SPECIFY: follow-up time points]

**Outcome 6: Relapse/Recurrence Rate**

## Effect Measures

### Primary Outcome: Depression Symptom Severity

**Outcome type**: Continuous

**Effect measure used**: **Standardized Mean Difference (SMD; Hedges' g)**

**Justification**: Depression symptom severity is measured across many scales (PHQ-9, BDI-II, HDRS, MADRS) with different scoring ranges and metrics. Standardized mean difference (g) is the appropriate metric for meta-analyzing continuous outcomes measured on different scales, allowing studies using different instruments to be pooled. Hedges' g is preferred over Cohen's d as it corrects for small sample bias.

**Calculation**: For each study, effect size will be calculated as:
- g = (M_intervention − M_comparator) / SD_pooled
- Where SD_pooled = √[((n1−1)SD1² + (n2−1)SD2²) / (n1+n2−2)]

**Interpretation of effect size magnitude** (following Cohen's conventions, as adapted for psychotherapy research):
- g ≈ 0.0–0.2: Trivial or small effect
- g ≈ 0.2–0.5: Small effect
- g ≈ 0.5–0.8: Medium/Moderate effect
- g > 0.8: Large effect

**Direction**: Positive values indicate CBT is superior to comparator (greater symptom reduction); negative values indicate comparator superior.

### Secondary Outcomes: Remission and Response Rates

**Outcome type**: Binary

**Effect measures used**:
1. **Risk Ratio (RR)** as the primary measure: RR = Proportion with remission in CBT arm / Proportion with remission in comparator arm
2. **Odds Ratio (OR)** as sensitivity/secondary measure

**Justification**: RR is more interpretable clinically than OR for trials with reasonable event rates (10–90%); it directly conveys the relative probability of remission with CBT. OR will be calculated for sensitivity analysis and when RR assumptions are violated (very low or very high event rates).

**Interpretation**:
- RR = 1.0: No difference between groups
- RR > 1.0: CBT superior (higher remission rate)
- RR < 1.0: Comparator superior
- Clinical significance threshold: RR ≥ 1.1–1.2 generally considered clinically meaningful; [SPECIFY: threshold for this review if differs]

**Calculation**: For each study: RR = [a/(a+b)] / [c/(c+d)], where a = remission events in CBT, b = non-remission in CBT, c = remission in comparator, d = non-remission in comparator

### Other Continuous Secondary Outcomes

**Quality of life, functional impairment, dropout rate** (if meta-analyzed as continuous outcomes):
- **Effect measure**: Standardized Mean Difference (Hedges' g), following same interpretation as above

### Binary Secondary Outcomes

**Adverse events, relapse/recurrence rates** (if meta-analyzed):
- **Effect measure**: Risk Ratio (RR) with interpretation as above; Risk Difference also calculated if event rates differ substantially between outcomes

### Time-to-Event Outcomes

**If relapse/recurrence data are presented as time-to-event**:
- **Effect measure**: Hazard Ratio (HR), reported with 95% CI if available from study

### 13b: Data Preparation and Transformation

#### Converting Outcomes to Common Metric: Depression Symptom Severity
**Challenge**: Studies measure depression using different scales (PHQ-9, 0–27 range; BDI-II, 0–63 range; HDRS, 0–52 range; MADRS, 0–60 range).

**Solution**: All studies will be converted to **Standardized Mean Difference (SMD; Hedges' g)**, which expresses treatment effect in units of pooled standard deviation, rendering scales directly comparable.

**Process**:
1. Extract or calculate means and SDs for CBT and comparator groups at baseline and endpoint
2. Calculate within-group change scores: MD = M_endpoint − M_baseline
3. Calculate pooled SD across both groups at baseline (to standardize change scores)
4. Derive SMD using the pooled SD: g = (MD_CBT − MD_comparator) / SD_pooled

**When direct data unavailable**:
- If only endpoint means reported: Use endpoint means directly (assuming pre-post correlations comparable across studies)
- If only medians available: Request raw data from authors; if unavailable, [SPECIFY: convert median to mean using study-specific approximations]

#### Deriving Variance Measures

**If studies report outcome data as**:
- **SEM or SE reported**: SD = SE × √N or SD = SEM × √N
- **95% CI reported**: SD = (Upper CI − Lower CI) / (1.96 × 2)
- **p-value and group means reported**: Derive SD using inverse normal function applied to t-statistic derived from p-value and group means
- **I.Q.R. or range reported**: Use transformation formulas: SD ≈ (Q3 − Q1) / 1.35 or SD ≈ Range / 4 (for normal distribution)

**Documentation**: The source of each variance estimate will be recorded (directly reported, calculated, imputed, or requested from authors).

#### Binary Outcomes: Event Rates for Meta-Analysis
**For remission and response rates**:
- Extract or calculate numerator (number with remission) and denominator (number analyzed) for each group
- If proportions reported without raw numbers: Multiply proportion × total N to derive cell counts
- If only "% with remission" reported: Contact authors for raw counts

#### Multi-Arm Trials: Handling Multiple Comparators
**Challenge**: Some trials compare CBT to multiple comparators (e.g., CBT vs. Standard Care AND CBT vs. Waitlist in same trial).

**Solution**:
- If both comparators meet inclusion criteria and are analyzed separately: Each comparison will be included as a separate analysis "sub-study" from the parent trial, with [SPECIFY: shared control group approach used — reduce the comparator group size proportionally to avoid double-counting in meta-analysis, OR include both comparisons with notation that they are not independent]
- Example: If a trial has 100 CBT participants, 100 Standard Care, and 100 Waitlist, rather than including two analyses with 100 CBT vs. 100 SC and 100 CBT vs. 100 WL (double-counting CBT), the CBT arm will be split (50 vs. 50 SC, 50 vs. 50 WL) OR the shared control group method will be used as implemented in meta-analysis software

#### Cluster-Randomized Trials

**If cluster-RCTs are identified** (e.g., therapists or clinics randomized rather than individual participants):
- **Design effect calculation**: The effective sample size will be adjusted using the formula: n_eff = N / (1 + (m − 1) × ICC), where m = average cluster size and ICC = intraclass correlation
- **ICC assumption**: If ICC not reported, [SPECIFY: assume ICC = 0.01 / use ICC from similar psychotherapy trials / contact authors for cluster-specific ICC]
- **Software implementation**: [SPECIFY: meta-analysis software used — e.g., RevMan, metafor in R] will apply the design effect correction

### 13f: Sensitivity Analyses

#### Planned Sensitivity Analyses
Sensitivity analyses will test whether pooled findings are robust to methodological decisions:

**1. Excluding studies at high risk of bias**
- **Decision tested**: Does inclusion of high-risk-of-bias studies substantially bias pooled estimate?

**2. Excluding studies with high attrition/missing data**
- **Decision tested**: Does including studies with >20% dropout bias results?
- **Approach**: Repeat meta-analysis excluding studies with [SPECIFY: >20% / >30%] attrition rates

- **Outcome**: Assess impact on effect size and heterogeneity

**4. Alternative effect measure for binary outcomes**
- **Decision tested**: Does choice of effect measure (RR vs. OR) affect conclusions?
- **Approach**: 
  - Primary analysis: Risk Ratio
  - Sensitivity: Odds Ratio meta-analysis
- **Outcome**: Compare results and interpretation

**5. Alternative statistical model**
- **Decision tested**: Does random-effects assumption vs. fixed-effect affect conclusions?
- **Approach**:
  - Primary: Random-effects model with [SPECIFY: estimator]
  - Sensitivity: Fixed-effect model
- **Outcome**: Assess changes in point estimate and CI width

**6. Excluding outlier studies**
- **Decision tested**: Does a single study with extreme effect size drive heterogeneity?
- **Approach**: 
  - Identify studies with effect sizes >2 SD from pooled mean
  - Repeat meta-analysis excluding outliers

- **Outcome**: If I² substantially decreases and effect estimate changes minimally, outlier has limited impact

- **Outcome**: Determine if criterion choice affects conclusions

#### Robustness Classification
**A finding will be classified as "robust" if**:
1. The pooled effect estimate direction and statistical significance (p < 0.05) remain consistent across sensitivity analyses, AND
2. The magnitude of pooled effect size change is <20% across analyses, AND
3. 95% confidence intervals substantially overlap across analyses (not excluding a clinically meaningful difference)

**A finding will be classified as "sensitive" if**:
1. Exclusion of particular studies or use of different assumptions substantially changes the pooled estimate direction or significance, OR
2. Heterogeneity is high and findings differ markedly across subgroups, OR
3. Confidence intervals change from excluding to including null effect with different assumptions

## GRADE Setup

### Framework Selection
**Grading of Recommendations Assessment, Development and Evaluation (GRADE)** framework will be used to assess the certainty of evidence for primary outcomes and key secondary outcomes.

**Justification**: GRADE is the current gold-standard approach for evaluating confidence in effect estimates across randomized trials, recommended by PRISMA 2020, and increasingly expected by clinical guideline developers.

### GRADE Domains Assessed

For each outcome, evidence will be assessed across the following domains:

**1. Risk of Bias** (Downgrade: serious or very serious concerns)
- Assessment method: Based on RoB 2 assessment conducted under Item 8
- Specific concerns:
  - Selection bias (randomization, allocation concealment failures)
  - Performance bias (therapist/participant blinding not possible for psychotherapy, but monitored)
  - Detection bias (assessor blinding for outcomes)
  - Attrition bias (differential dropout affecting completeness of outcome data)
  - Reporting bias (selective reporting of outcomes or time points)
- **Downgrading rule**: 
  - If ≥50% of studies contributing to outcome have high risk of bias in critical domains → Serious concern (downgrade 1 level)
  - If ≥75% of studies have high risk of bias → Very serious concern (downgrade 2 levels)

**2. Inconsistency** (Downgrade: serious or very serious concerns)
- Assessment method: Examination of heterogeneity (I², subgroup analyses, prediction intervals)
- **Downgrading rule**:
  - I² = 50–75% (moderate heterogeneity) AND confidence intervals from individual studies show divergent directions or effects → Serious concern (downgrade 1 level)
  - I² > 75% (high heterogeneity) OR individual study CIs consistently fail to overlap → Very serious concern (downgrade 2 levels)
  - If subgroup analyses explain heterogeneity (interaction p < 0.05 and I² within subgroups < 50%), do not downgrade for inconsistency (heterogeneity is explained)

**3. Indirectness** (Downgrade: serious or very serious concerns)
- Assessment of whether included studies directly address the review question regarding population, intervention, comparator, and outcomes

  - Significant population, intervention, or outcome mismatch in most studies → Serious concern (downgrade 1 level)
  - Majority of evidence indirectly addresses question → Very serious concern (downgrade 2 levels)
  - No indirectness → No downgrade

**4. Imprecision** (Downgrade: serious or very serious concerns)
- Assessment of whether the pooled estimate has adequate precision (95% CI narrow enough to exclude clinically unimportant effects)
- **Clinical decision threshold**: The pooled effect must exclude a clinically trivial effect to demonstrate adequate precision
- **Specific rules for this review**:
  - For depression symptom severity (SMD): Clinically important difference = [SPECIFY: SMD 0.3–0.5; if CI includes only trivial effects <0.3 or includes both clinically meaningful and trivial ranges] → Serious imprecision (downgrade 1 level)
  - For remission/response rate (RR): Clinically important difference = [SPECIFY: RR 1.0–1.2; if wide CI includes both benefit and harm or crosses 1.0] → Serious imprecision (downgrade 1 level)
  - Very small number of studies (k < 3) → Downgrade for imprecision even if CI narrow (reflects sparse data)
  - Sample size substantially less than optimal information size (calculated a priori) → Downgrade 1 level

**5. Publication Bias** (Downgrade: serious concerns)
- Assessment method: Funnel plot visual inspection and Egger's test; [SPECIFY: trim-and-fill analysis if detected]
- **Specific rule**:
  - Visual funnel plot asymmetry (larger effect in smaller studies) + Egger's test p < 0.10 (or other evidence of selective reporting) → Serious publication bias concern (downgrade 1 level)
  - Small number of studies (k < 10) → Do not assess publication bias formally (asymmetry assessment unreliable); note as limitation
  - Symmetrical funnel plot and non-significant Egger's test → No downgrade

### Upgrading Domains (Rare in Psychotherapy Trials)
**Evidence may be upgraded if**:
1. **Large effect size**: Effect size substantially larger than expected (e.g., SMD > 1.0) in context of expected treatment effect → Upgrade 1 level (if no other serious concerns)
2. **Dose-response gradient**: Evidence of relationship between intervention "dose" (session frequency, duration) and effect magnitude → Upgrade 1 level
3. **Opposing confounding**: Evidence that unmeasured confounders would likely bias against the observed effect (e.g., CBT group more treatment-resistant at baseline) → Upgrade 1 level if bias would favor comparator

**For this review, upgrading is anticipated to be uncommon** given the typical effect sizes for psychotherapy and expected design limitations.

### GRADE Assessment Process

**Number of reviewers**: Two independent assessors will evaluate GRADE domains for each outcome

**Assessment approach**: 
- Assessor 1: Independently assesses each domain and assigns provisional GRADE rating (High, Moderate, Low, or Very Low certainty)
- Assessor 2: Independently assesses; comparison with Assessor 1
- Disagreement resolution: Initial discrepancies discussed; if unresolved after discussion, senior methodologist consulted for final rating

**Certainty rating assignment** (based on aggregate downgrading/upgrading):
- **High certainty**: RCT evidence with no serious limitations; confidence that effect is true and further research is unlikely to change the effect estimate substantially
- **Moderate certainty**: RCT evidence with some limitations; confidence in effect estimate is warranted, but effects may change with additional research
- **Low certainty**: RCT evidence with serious limitations; effects may be substantially different from true effect; more research needed
- **Very Low certainty**: Very serious limitations; effect estimate highly uncertain; estimate may differ substantially from true effect; very little confidence in findings
