# Phase 2 Output — Introduction

## Rationale

Major depressive disorder (MDD) remains one of the leading causes of disability and reduced quality of life globally, with substantial economic burden in healthcare systems and reduced work productivity. While cognitive behavioral therapy (CBT) has been established as a first-line psychological intervention for depression and is recommended in clinical practice guidelines, the strength and consistency of evidence supporting its efficacy varies considerably across diverse clinical contexts. Published trials of CBT for depression employ heterogeneous comparison conditions (including standard care, placebo, other psychotherapies, and waitlist controls), outcome measurement scales, intervention delivery formats, and patient populations, yet a comprehensive and current synthesis of this evidence has not been systematically assembled.

The existing literature lacks a consolidated analysis of CBT's relative efficacy compared to multiple comparator conditions simultaneously, and critical evidence gaps remain regarding the effectiveness of different CBT delivery modalities (face-to-face versus digital/teletherapy), the durability of treatment effects (remission and sustained response versus symptom reduction), and variation in outcomes across patient subgroups defined by depression severity and treatment setting. Clinical decision-makers and patients require synthesized evidence about both the magnitude of CBT's effect and which comparator interventions represent the most appropriate alternatives across different clinical scenarios. A systematic review and meta-analysis of randomized controlled trials published since 2010 will provide current, high-quality evidence on these questions and clarify the evidence base for clinical practice.

## Objectives

This systematic review and meta-analysis aims to evaluate the efficacy of cognitive behavioral therapy (CBT) for major depressive disorder in adults by synthesizing evidence from randomized controlled trials. 

**Primary research question**: In adults aged 18 years and older with major depressive disorder, what is the effect of cognitive behavioral therapy compared to standard care, placebo/attention control, other psychotherapies, or waitlist control on depression symptom severity, remission rates, and response rates?

**PICO Framework**:
- **Population**: Adults aged ≥18 years with a diagnosis of major depressive disorder (DSM-5 or ICD-11 criteria), of any severity level, across any treatment setting
- **Intervention**: Cognitive behavioral therapy, delivered individually or in groups, via any delivery method (face-to-face, teletherapy, or digital), of any duration and frequency
- **Comparators**: (1) Standard/usual care, (2) Placebo or attention control, (3) Other structured psychotherapies (psychodynamic, interpersonal, or supportive therapy), or (4) Waitlist control
- **Outcomes**: 
  - Primary: Depression symptom severity (measured by validated instruments: PHQ-9, BDI-II, HDRS, or equivalent), remission rates (≥50% reduction or full remission from baseline), and response rates
  - Secondary: Quality of life, functional impairment, relapse/recurrence rates, treatment dropout rates, and adverse events

**Study design**: Randomized controlled trials published in English from 2010 onwards with a minimum sample size of 20 participants.

**Planned comparisons**: Meta-analyses will be stratified by comparator type, and subgroup analyses will examine effect modification by CBT delivery format (face-to-face vs. digital), depression severity at baseline, treatment setting, and publication era.
